Too Little, Too Late, and Not Enough, Von der Leyen

September 9, 2025

On September 9th, when European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen gave her State of the Union address to the European Parliament, she made a bold claim about the Israeli-Palestine conflict...

The EU is no stranger to radical policies that require bold initiatives. The European Green Deal and the EU’s policy towards refugees are among the most progressive in the world...

The European Commission, whilst the highest body with executive political power in the EU, does not have legislative authority to enact laws. Instead, they must send proposals to the Council of the European Union, which must negotiate with the European Parliament to adopt EU legislation. Given the controversy, the Commission is seeking a resolution instead of full lawmaking, requiring approval from the Council of the EU under the qualified majority voting system. Whether it passes remains uncertain, and even then, the measures are limited.

The proposed tariffs only cover about 37% of Israeli exports to the EU, leaving most trade untouched. A Commission official admitted that tariffs would not affect capital movements because the EU does not want to “punish” Israel. Yet, symbolic tariffs risk undermining the EU’s credibility as a defender of human rights. The Treaty on the European Union calls for fair trade, poverty eradication, and human rights protection...

If the EU wants to get serious about leveraging its power, it must consider harsher, more concrete punishments. Suspending the EU–Israel Association Agreement would strip Israel of trade benefits... An arms embargo would also be significant...
