December 7th 2nd

The United Nations Genocide Convention defines genocide as the “intent to destroy, in whole or in part, a national, ethnical, racial or religious group” by way of committing any of the following against the group(s): “killing; causing serious bodily or mental harm; deliberately inflicting…conditions of life calculated to bring about its physical destruction in whole or in part; imposing measures intended to prevent births; forcibly transferring children…to another group” (United Nations, 1948, art. II). The next article expands that not only will genocide be punishable, but “conspiracy to commit genocide; dire and public incitement to commit genocide; attempt to commit genocide; complicity in genocide” (United Nations, 1948, art. III). After entering into force in January of 1952, 153 states have become party to the treaty as of February of this year, with only one signature, the Dominican Republic, not ratifying it, and forty-four that have yet to sign or ratify at all.

Crimes against humanity, are “acts committed as part of a widespread or systematic attack directed against any civilian population, such as: murder; extermination; deportation or forcible transfer of population; torture; rape…or any other form of sexual violence; the crime of apartheid; other inhumane acts…causing great suffering, or serious injury to body or to mental or physical health” (Global Centre for the Responsibility to Protect, 2018, “Crimes Against Humanity” section). What differentiates genocide from crimes against humanity is “proven intent…to physically destroy a national, ethnic, racial, or religious group,” with genocide victims being “deliberately, not randomly, targeted because of their real or perceived membership in one of the four protected groups” (Global Centre for the Responsibility to Protect, 2018, “Genocide” section). Genocide and crimes against humanity can happen at any time, both in and out of armed conflicts, whereas war crimes can only occur during armed conflicts and wars.
